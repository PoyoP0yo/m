## Purpose

Landing a role as a Power Systems Engineer after graduation requires more than strong grades — recruiters
in this field are looking for candidates who can demonstrate practical skill and professional engagement.
[^1] This guide walks you through the best practices you should follow
from your first year through graduation to build the technical portfolio, credentials, and network that
make you competitive. Starting early matters. Every practice that is outlined here builds up over time.
Students who begin in their first or second year consistently have more to show than those who wait. Follow
these steps and you will graduate ready to apply and not be scrambling to catch up.

## Building Your Technical Foundation

### Mastering Industry Tools

Power systems roles consistently call for proficiency in simulation and analysis software in tools like
PSCAD, PSS/E, PSSE, DigSilent PowerFactory, and EasyPower appear repeatedly in job postings for
entry-level and internship candidates.[^2] Your coursework will introduce some of these, but recruiters
distinguish candidates who have used these tools independently from those who only touched them in a
lab setting. Working through at least one simulation tool outside of class for instance using an open-source trial or
student licenses to model a small distribution or microgrid system, document your process, and save
your project files. That saved work becomes the first piece of your portfolio. Python is also increasingly
expected. You could use it to automate a power flow calculation or visualize load data from a public dataset so
you have a concrete coding sample to showcase.[^2]

### Pursuing Certification Early

![Alternative text](https://github.com/MarisaSipaseuth/ProcessFigure/blob/main/README.md)


The Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) exam is the first formal step toward a Professional Engineer (PE)
license and the best time to take it is during your senior year or within a few months of
graduation. This is when engineering knowledge is freshest at that point, and waiting makes the exam
significantly harder as coursework fades.[^3] Passing the FE starts the four-year experience clock
required before you can sit for the PE exam; the earlier you pass, the sooner you become eligible
for full licensure.[^3] The PE Electrical: Power exam is offered year-round through NCEES, so
delaying the FE simply delays your entire license timeline.[^4]

Join IEEE and add the Power & Energy Society (PES) student membership — the add-on costs as little
as $1 per year for existing IEEE student members.[^5] PES membership gives you access to technical
publications, conference discounts, scholarship opportunities like the PES Scholarship Plus
Initiative, and mentorship through PES Student and Young Professionals chapters.[^5] These
connections directly support your technical development and your network at the same time.

## Demonstrating Your Skills Outside the Classroom

### Getting Hands-On Experience

Your coursework grounds your academic experience, but recruiters for power systems roles want evidence
that you understand the real challenges of building and operating a system.[^1] Pursue internships or
co-ops at electric utilities, engineering consulting firms, or grid operators as early as your
sophomore or junior year — even a single semester of relevant experience signals to hiring managers
that you can function in a professional environment. If no internship is available, look for
undergraduate research opportunities in your department's power group; co-authoring even one paper
or technical report is a strong differentiator.[^2]

Participate in extracurricular activities that generate real engineering artifacts: joining your
university's IEEE PES student chapter gives you access to technical workshops and industry speaker
events, and taking on a leadership role within the chapter demonstrates the project management and
communication skills that employers consistently cite as critical.[^6] Community or volunteer projects
that involve electrical systems — such as assisting with solar installations for local organizations —
are also worth documenting on your resume.[^1]

### Building a Power Systems Portfolio

A portfolio proves what a resume can only claim. Create a GitHub repository or a simple personal
website and add your simulation projects, class design work, and any independent analyses you have
completed. Each project entry should explain what problem you solved, which tools you used, and what
the result was — three to four sentences is enough. Recruiters in power systems will check whether
your work reflects the skills listed on your resume, so having public, viewable evidence closes
that gap before the interview even starts.[^2]

## Breaking Into the Industry

### Networking with Power Systems Professionals

Power systems is a field where professional relationships significantly influence hiring. Many
entry-level openings at utilities and consulting firms are filled through referrals and internship
pipelines rather than open postings.[^1] Attend the IEEE PES General Meeting or regional PES
conferences while you are still a student; student rates are discounted, and these events put you
in the same room as the engineers who will eventually be your hiring managers or references.[^5]
Connect with speakers and panelists on LinkedIn within 24 hours of meeting them, and send a
short, specific note referencing the conversation. Often times generic connection requests get ignored.
Your informational interview contact is a ready-made starting point: follow up, stay in touch,
and ask for introductions to two or three of their colleagues in power systems roles.

### Preparing for Technical Interviews

Power systems interviews typically include both behavioral questions and technical rounds that test
your ability to apply power flow, protection, and circuit analysis concepts under time pressure.[^2]
Practice your behavioral answers using the SARI format (Situation, Action, Result, Impact) — this
structure keeps answers focused and makes your individual contribution clear. For the technical
round, review fault analysis, load flow calculations, and protection coordination, and be ready to
work through a simple single-line diagram problem on a whiteboard. The students who struggle in
technical interviews are typically those who have only solved problems in structured homework
contexts; working through problems from memory under time pressure requires separate practice,
so build that into your preparation at least one semester before you start recruiting.
